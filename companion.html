The Companion Guide

Twelve months of gentle practice — for the grown-up, not the child.

This guide is built on the developmental science of Alison Gopnik (The Scientist in the Crib, The Gardener and the Carpenter). Its central idea: a young child isn't clay to be shaped toward a target. She's already a little scientist, running experiments on the world. Your job isn't to train her — it's to be rich soil she can grow in.

So there is no curriculum for her. Each month gives you one stance to practise. She just plays and responds; that response is all the success there is. Move at her pace, not the calendar's — the months are a suggestion, and repeating one is fine.

How to use it

Read the month you're on. Try the invitations when they fit into ordinary time together — none of them need special materials or a set-aside "lesson." The Watch for notes tell you what her learning looks like as it happens, so you can see it rather than test for it. When you notice something worth remembering, jot it down — it's how each child's plan becomes their own.

A closing note

If a month doesn't land, let it go and follow what she's actually doing. The whole point of the gardener's approach is that she's growing herself — the best thing you bring is warm, unhurried attention. That you're doing this at all is the thing that matters most.

This guide offers the general research picture, not advice tailored to one child. For anything specific to her development, her parents and pediatrician are the right people to ask.
