# Research note — bilingualism in the early years (mixed-language households)

> Raw source. Web-validated 2026-07 (see Sources). Motivating context: many
> households — including mixed-tradition families — expose a child to two
> languages/cultures from birth. This note grounds the dyad model's dual
> culture/language tracks and the bilingual-first-words feature. No names;
> general science only.

## Key findings (mainstream consensus)

- **Infants distinguish languages within days-to-months of birth.** A
  two-language household does not confuse a baby; bilingual infants actively
  manage and separate their languages as they listen (PNAS: "Bilingual infants
  control their languages as they listen").
- **Milestones arrive on schedule.** Babbling and first words appear on roughly
  the same timeline as monolinguals. Count vocabulary **across both languages**
  when judging progress — per-language counts understate a bilingual child.
- **Exposure quantity & quality drive outcomes.** Birth-to-~3 is a sensitive
  window; success in each language tracks the amount of rich, *live*,
  interactive input the child receives in it (home, family, community).
- **Code-mixing is normal bilingual behavior**, not evidence of confusion.
  Nuance: one toddler study associated heavy in-sentence mixing by parents with
  smaller vocabularies — so model the "name it warmly in both, one at a time"
  style ("dog… kutta!") rather than blended sentences.
- **Social gating applies (Kuhl).** Infants learn a language's sounds from a
  live, responsive person — not from audio or video. Already in
  `books/scientist-in-the-crib.md`; it is load-bearing here.

## Design implications for this project

1. **The app must never position itself as the language teacher.** The grown-up
   speaking on the lap is the mechanism; the app prompts and supports (this is
   why bilingual-first-words has the grown-up say both words — no synthetic
   pronunciation).
2. **Dyad profiles carry up to two culture/language tracks** (mixed households:
   two traditions, two languages). Content can alternate/blend: festivals,
   foods, animals, words from both sides.
3. **Cues model clean alternation**, not blended sentences, per the code-mixing
   nuance.
4. Progress narration should treat both languages as one growing vocabulary.
