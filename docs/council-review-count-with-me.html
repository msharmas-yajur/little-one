The Council in Session

The complete conversation from the Review Council's first escalation — six independent LLM-judge seats reviewing the Mechanic's first proposal, the count game (count-with-me), and sending it to the human. This page is the transcript teams should read to understand how the panel actually behaves.

2026-07-08 · artifact wordgame/count-with-me · proposed by the Mechanic (PR #3) · CI run 28868285280 · permanent record: knowledge-base/ledger/council/2026-07-08-count-with-me.md

What was on the table

The Mechanic loop's first-ever proposal: a new game type, count — the engine shows a small set (2–4) of one picture and the original prompt asked "How many ducks? Tap each one!"; each tap counts aloud (one, two, three…) and the total is cheered. It had already passed the two deterministic gates (schema validation, JS capability lint). The owner chose Council-first review before merging.

How the panel works: six seats, each with a different constitution compiled from the knowledge base, judge independently — they never see each other's verdicts. A Chairman synthesises but cannot vote. The publish/revise/escalate decision is computed deterministically from the votes; the Guardian's hold can never be overridden by any number of approvals.

🔴 Decision: ESCALATE

The Guardian Seat did not approve — escalates to the owner (the Guardian's hold cannot be overridden).

🧭 Explorer — reject 🎯 Skills — revise 🗣️ Voice — revise 💛 Tone — revise 🛡️ Guardian — reject 🛋️ Lap — revise

Chairman's synthesis

The mechanical decision is escalate: the Guardian did not approve, and by charter its hold cannot be overridden by any number of approvals. This word-game goes to you, not back to the generator.

The council is strikingly aligned on the core problem. Five of six seats independently flagged the same fault: the 'count' mode's 'How many ducks? Tap each one!' prompt is a quiz with a right answer. Explorer and Guardian call this fatal and reject; Skills, Voice, Tone, and Lap call it fixable and ask for a revise. The convergence matters — when seats grounded in different constitutions (child-as-explorer, no-fail design, never-test tone, the lap dyad) all land on the same line independently, that is the strongest signal the council can produce.

The Guardian adds two structural blocks that only the owner can clear: (1) the artifact targets ages 2–4, outside the founding fact that fixes the little one at 1 year old with an arc forward; and (2) 'count' is a new mechanic, and new mechanics are class-level decisions reserved to you, never the council's to approve. The Guardian also notes it could not verify the art keys against the registry and PII is clean.

Secondary, honest notes: Skills says the focus tag is defensible but critical-thinking over-claims — counting exercises one-to-one correspondence, closer to Making Connections, not the cause-and-effect prediction the KB defines as Critical Thinking. Voice flags there is no grown-up cue: line at all: the prompt speaks to the child, positioning the app as the counting voice — a social-gating inversion — with no serve-and-return pause.

In short: even setting aside the reframe question, this cannot publish. Two class-level decisions (the age band and the 'count' mechanic) are yours alone, and the deterministic art-key check has not been run.

Key disagreements the Chairman surfaced

  • Reject vs. revise on the same fault. Explorer and Guardian treat the right-answer counting mechanic as a categorical exclusion (carpenter drilling / no-fail red-line) and reject; Skills, Voice, Tone, and Lap treat it as salvageable copy that one bounce could fix. This is the difference between 'redesign the mechanic' and 'reword the prompt' — but moot for the decision, since the Guardian's hold escalates regardless.
  • What counting is, developmentally. Skills reads the mechanic as one-to-one correspondence/quantity (Making Connections at most, not Critical Thinking), while the generator tagged it critical-thinking. A live question about which skill, if any, a counting task honestly exercises.
  • Scope of the age concern. Guardian and Lap treat age 2–4 as a hard conflict with the 1-year-old founding fact; Skills abstains as out-of-lens; the tone/voice seats don't raise it. Only the Guardian frames it as a blocking class decision — which is the one that governs.

🧭 Explorer's Seat — ❌ reject

Constitution: Gopnik — the child as explorer; gardener, not carpenter

A fixed-count 'how many, tap each one' mechanic is a carpenter drilling toward a target answer, which my constitution explicitly excludes as testing/drilling rather than open-ended, child-led exploration.
Cited: gopnik-foundation.md — "child as explorer… a shared learning experience, not testing or drilling"; gopnik-foundation.md — "The adult's job is to let that play run and put words to it, not to correct or redirect"; gardener-and-the-carpenter.md — "A carpenter works from a blueprint, shaping raw material toward a fixed outcome"; gardener-and-the-carpenter.md — "no quizzing, no goal but warmth and presence"
  • The 'count' mode with a fixed count per round encodes a single correct answer — this is a quiz/drill toward a target, the carpenter's blueprint, not open-ended exploration.
  • 'How many ducks? Tap each one!' directs the child toward a right-answer procedure rather than following the child's own lantern focus; the adult becomes an examiner, not a co-explorer.
  • The mechanic treats the child as raw material to be shaped toward numeracy outcomes, contradicting the arc's core ethos that it is a practice of warmth/presence, not a child curriculum with measurable outputs.
  • Nothing here invites the child to drop, repeat, compare, or discover on her own terms — the interaction is closed and outcome-scored.
  • Reframe from 'how many / tap each one' to open naming and following the child's focus (e.g., point-and-name whatever she fixates on, no target count), so the grown-up puts words to her play rather than testing recall.
  • If counting is desired, present it as the grown-up modeling counting aloud once and then letting the child bang/tap/re-discover freely, with no correct-count gate — matching the 'model once, then let her explore' principle.
  • Remove the implicit right-answer scoring entirely; ensure no round can be 'failed' or completed 'wrong.'

🎯 Skills Seat — 🟡 revise

Constitution: Galinsky — the seven essential life skills; honest tagging

The focus tag is honest but the critical-thinking tag over-claims — a counting/tapping game does not exercise the cause-and-effect, prediction-testing 'search for reliable knowledge' that my constitution defines as Critical Thinking.
Cited: seven-life-skills.md (#5 Critical Thinking); mind-in-the-making.md (Critical Thinking → cause-and-effect play; Focus & Self-Control); seven-life-skills.md (#1 Focus & Self-Control)
  • The critical-thinking tag is not honest against my constitution. Critical Thinking in the KB is 'the ongoing search for reliable knowledge' exercised through cause-and-effect play (peekaboo, drop-and-retrieve, lift-the-flap) and anticipating what happens next. A fixed-count tap-and-count game exercises one-to-one correspondence and quantity, not cause-and-effect reasoning — nothing here surprises, tests a prediction, or rewards a search for reliable knowledge. The tag over-claims.
  • The focus tag IS defensible: 'Tap each one' requires sustained attention and tracking which items have been counted — Focus & Self-Control (attention, remembering, resisting distraction). This tag can stand.
  • Counting is closer to Making Connections (same/different, sorting, quantity linking) than to Critical Thinking — the generator may have reached for the wrong tag.
  • Out of lens: the age (2+) sits outside the 12-month arc my constitution is written around — I abstain on age-appropriateness and defer to the Guardian/Lap seats.
  • Drop the critical-thinking tag — it is not exercised by a static counting task as defined in seven-life-skills.md.
  • If a second tag is wanted, consider connections (skill #4: sorting, same/different, quantity linking) — a more honest fit for counting — OR redesign a round to genuinely exercise critical thinking (e.g. a hidden/appearing item that lets the child anticipate the next count).
  • Keep the focus tag — it is honestly exercised by the tap-each-one mechanic.

🗣️ Voice Seat — 🟡 revise

Constitution: responsive talk, social gating — the grown-up is the language mechanism

No bilingual claims are made so nothing is dishonest there, but the game has no grown-up cue and no serve-and-return, and its prompt positions the app as the counting voice — a social-gating failure that requires revision.
Cited: responsive-talk.md (cue lines must model descriptive talk and serve-and-return pauses; a good cue tells the grown-up what to say AND to wait for the child's turn); bilingual-early-years.md (social gating / Kuhl: the grown-up on the lap is the language mechanism; the app must never position itself as the teacher)
  • The artifact contains no grown-up cue: line at all. The prompt ('How many ducks? Tap each one!') speaks directly to the child, positioning the app — not the grown-up — as the voice conducting the game. This inverts the social-gating principle: the grown-up should be the mechanism, the app should prompt the grown-up.
  • No serve-and-return is present or supported anywhere: no built-in pause, no direction to the grown-up to answer the child's count/point and then wait. The counting mode as written is a child-to-screen tap loop — the exact anti-pattern social gating warns against.
  • 'Tap each one!' directs the child to interact with the device rather than with the grown-up, further sidelining the dyad.
  • Add a grown-up cue: line for each round that models descriptive, serve-and-return talk (e.g., 'Point to each duck together — "one… two…" — then wait for the little one to try.') so the grown-up voices the counting, not the app.
  • Reframe the prompt as an instruction to the grown-up rather than a command to the child, keeping the grown-up as the language mechanism.
  • Add an explicit pause / wait-for-the-turn beat so the claimed interaction is genuinely serve-and-return, not tap-response.

💛 Tone Seat — 🟡 revise

Constitution: Faber & King — describe don't judge; never test

The counting prompt is structured as a test with a correct answer, which directly violates the never-test / describe-don't-judge principles; it can be salvaged by reframing to descriptive, feelings-first narration on one bounce.
Cited: how-to-talk-little-kids.md — "Keep cues warm and non-corrective (never test)" / "Describe, don't judge"; responsive-talk.md — "Describe, don't judge", "Acknowledge feelings first", "Serve and return … then wait"
  • The core prompt 'How many {label}? Tap each one!' is framed as a quiz with a single correct answer — it tests the child, which my constitution explicitly forbids. A right/wrong count implies an implicit fail state if the child taps the wrong number.
  • No descriptive-talk modeling: there is no grown-up cue that reports what the child actually does ('You tapped the duck!') rather than judging correctness — misses the house style that all cue lines should be descriptive, not evaluative.
  • No feelings-first acknowledgement and no serve-and-return pause is built into the prompt; the artifact drives toward an answer rather than honoring the child's turn.
  • Note on scope: 'count' mode / age 2+ band may be a new mechanic-class question outside my lens — flagging for the owner/Guardian, not approving it here.
  • Reframe the prompt from an evaluative question to descriptive, playful narration: cue the grown-up to name and point ('Look — a duck… and another duck!') and celebrate the tap descriptively ('You tapped the duck!') regardless of count, so there is no correct/incorrect outcome.
  • Add an explicit grown-up cue line that acknowledges feeling and models a serve-and-return pause ('Point together — then wait for the tap'), per responsive-talk.md.
  • Ensure the mechanic has no scorekeeping or fail branch for a 'wrong' count; every tap should be honored warmly.

🛡️ Guardian Seat — ❌ reject

Constitution: hard rules — privacy, founding fact, no-fail floor, class decisions. The Guardian's hold cannot be overridden.

The artifact targets ages 2–4 (outside the 1-year-old founding fact) and its count-to-a-target mechanic reads as a test with a right answer, which violates the no-fail/never-test design principle; both the age band and the new 'count' mode are also class decisions reserved to the owner.
Cited: knowledge-base/CLAUDE.md (the founding fact: the little one is 1 year old today); design-principles.md (no fail states / never test the child / the arc is a practice for the ADULT, not a curriculum); privacy-model.md (no PII — passes); COUNCIL.md hard rule: classes are never the Council's to approve
  • Age red-line: the founding fact fixes the little one at 1 year old with an arc forward; age_band 2-3y and 3-4y (age 2+) falls outside the project's scope and cannot be approved by the Guardian without owner sign-off.
  • Fail-state / testing risk: a 'count' mode asking 'How many ducks?' with a fixed target count implies a correct answer and a wrong answer — design-principles.md forbids pass/fail tasks, tests, and any curriculum-for-the-child framing. Nothing in the artifact specifies the forgiving, no-fail behavior for an incorrect count.
  • New mechanic: mode 'count' is not an approved existing mechanic — new mechanics/schema modes are a class decision reserved to the owner, not the council.
  • Art-key verification: I cannot deterministically confirm the art keys exist in the registry. This must pass the schema validator before any seat opines; I have no registry to check against here.
  • PII: none present — this point passes.
  • Escalate to owner: both the age band (2–4y) and the 'count' mode are class-level decisions outside council approval authority.
  • If a counting-style experience is pursued, redesign it to remove any correct/target count so it cannot function as a test — success must equal engagement, not a right answer.
  • Run the art keys through the deterministic allowlist/schema validator and attach the result before re-review.

🛋️ Lap Seat — 🟡 revise

Constitution: the 9pm reality — a tired grown-up, a squirming child, one lap

The rounds are short and the tap cue is lap-practical, but the 'How many? / count' framing turns bedtime reading into a quiz, which conflicts with the no-test, adult-stance-not-child-curriculum principles.
Cited: design-principles.md; the-12-month-arc.md
  • The prompt 'How many {label}? Tap each one!' frames a right-answer quiz. At 9pm a tired grown-up with a squirming child will read this as a test and be pulled toward correcting a wrong count — this cuts against 'the game is forgiving — no fail states… never test.' The counting-quiz cadence serves an abstract instructional goal, not the warm lap dyad.
  • Scope/rhythm: the artifact is age 2+, while the 12-month arc frames practices for the grown-up starting at age 1 as a shared stance, not a curriculum. A numeracy 'count' mechanic reads as curriculum-for-the-child rather than an adult stance, which the arc explicitly rejects.
  • Practicality is otherwise fine: five short rounds (counts 2,3,3,4,2) is a sensible length, and 'Tap each one!' is a concrete, big-target-friendly gesture — no length or motion objection here.
  • Reframe the prompt away from the quiz form ('How many?') toward a shared, unfalsifiable invitation, e.g. 'Let's tap the {label} together!' — so a wrong tap wobbles and delights rather than failing the child.
  • If a counting stance is intended, express it as a grown-up practice (a stance line the adult reads) per the 12-month arc, not as a scored task the child must get right.
  • Confirm the age band is intentional and that the mechanic still honours the no-test / no-fail floor for the older dyad; if kept at 2+, ensure the copy never implies a correct answer the parent must enforce.

⚖️ The owner's ruling: revise, then merge

The escalation went to the human — as designed. The full record lives at knowledge-base/ledger/decisions/2026-07-08-pr3.md, including what was adopted and what was overruled. Recording both is deliberate: the disagreements are calibration data for the judges.

Adopted from the seats

  • ✔ Prompt reframed from quiz to invitation — "Let's count the ducks — together!" replaces "How many ducks? Tap each one!" (the 5/6-seat convergence; the final copy is nearly the Lap Seat's exact suggestion).
  • ✔ Grown-up cue restored to the dyad (Voice Seat) — an optional per-round cue added to the schema and renderer, with five serve-and-return cues written: "You count aloud, the little one taps. Pause after each number — the pause is the turn."
  • ✔ Honest tags (Skills Seat) — critical-thinking → connections: counting is one-to-one correspondence, not cause-and-effect prediction.

The two class rulings (the Guardian's escalation grounds)

  • Forward age-bands (2–3y/3–4y) are in scope. The founding fact fixes where the little one is today; the schema and roadmap deliberately plan forward. Rubric note queued for COUNCIL.md: forward-band content is not an age red-line by itself.
  • The count mechanic is approved as a class. The owner's merge is the class decision the charter reserves.

Overruled — and why (this is the calibration data)

  • ✘ "Counting-to-a-target is a fail-state test" (Explorer/Guardian) — reviewed against the code, which the Council never sees, an incorrect count is impossible: the child never states a number; taps only ever count up; repeat taps are gentle no-ops. There is no input that can be wrong. The Council reviews the YAML artifact, not the renderer — a scope limit worth remembering when weighing categorical rejections.
  • ✘ "The app should not voice the counting" (Voice Seat, strongest form) — partially adopted. The app keeps its gentle spoken count-words, consistent with find-mode speaking labels everywhere else; the new cues make the grown-up the primary counting voice with the pause as the turn.
