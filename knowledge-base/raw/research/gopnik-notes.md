# Research note — Gopnik & the developmental basis

Source of the project's approach. From Alison Gopnik's work and mainstream
early-childhood science.

Key points:
- Warm, responsive relationships + unstructured play + language-rich everyday
  interaction + secure attachment do far more at this age than accelerated
  training. This is the field's broad consensus.
- "The child as explorer": babies learn by trying things out and repeating; the
  adult is good company for that, not a drill instructor. (The Scientist in the Crib.)
- "Gardener vs carpenter": tending a rich environment beats shaping toward a
  target. (The Gardener and the Carpenter.)
- Dialogic reading has the strongest evidence among concrete techniques: the
  adult prompts the child to tell the story, evaluates, expands, repeats (PEER).
  Maps onto the site's tap-and-name design.
- Caution on "training high-performing children": flashcard/hothousing methods
  tend to produce brief gains that fade, with opportunity cost (time not spent
  on the play and back-and-forth that actually help). Traits that predict later
  performance — persistence, curiosity, regulation — come from secure
  relationships and self-directed play.

Books worth citing: The Scientist in the Crib; The Gardener and the Carpenter
(Gopnik); Mind in the Making (Galinsky); How to Talk So Little Kids Will Listen.
Non-commercial sources: National Academies "Parenting Matters"; ZERO TO THREE.

Caveat: general research picture, not personalized advice. Child-specific
questions → pediatrician.
