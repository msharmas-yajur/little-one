# Book Foundations

The four books the 12-month arc draws on. Everything here is framed the project's
way: a shared **learning experience** for the little one and the grown-up, with
the **child's experience first** and the grown-up's practice in support of it.
(We use "the child as **explorer**," not the "scientist/experiment" metaphor.)

| Book | One-line contribution | Informs most |
|------|-----------------------|--------------|
| *The Scientist in the Crib* (Gopnik, Meltzoff, Kuhl) | Babies learn the world through play, imitation, and warm back-and-forth talk — not drilling or screens. | Months 1, 4, 6, 10 |
| *The Gardener and the Carpenter* (Gopnik) | Tend a rich, secure environment (gardener); don't shape toward a target (carpenter). The arc's ethos. | Months 2, 5, 12 |
| *Mind in the Making* (Galinsky) | Seven executive-function life skills, fostered through everyday interaction. Maps to monthly stances. | Months 1–11 (see [seven-life-skills](seven-life-skills.md)) |
| *How to Talk So Little Kids Will Listen* (Faber & King) | Acknowledge feelings first; cooperation through warmth, choice, play, descriptive praise. | Months 6–9; all grown-up cues |

## The child comes first
Each book, read for this project, points the same way: **follow the little one.**
The grown-up's job is warm attention and rich talk *around* what she is already
doing — not a lesson she must pass. The arc encodes this by making every month a
grown-up **stance**, never a target for the child.

## Where they agree (the load-bearing ideas)
- **Learning lives in relationship.** Face-to-face, responsive back-and-forth
  ("serve and return") is the mechanism — the shared lap, not the device.
  (*Scientist in the Crib*, *Mind in the Making*.)
- **Play and exploration beat instruction.** Let her lead, repeat, and re-discover;
  resist correcting the "right" use. (*Gardener and the Carpenter*.)
- **Name generously and warmly.** Rich talk, feelings included; describe rather
  than judge. (*Mind in the Making*, *How to Talk*.)
- **Security is the launchpad.** A predictable, loving ritual is what frees her to
  venture. (*Gardener and the Carpenter*.)

## How to use this for arc planning
When drafting or revising a month:
1. Start from the **child's experience** — what is she doing/enjoying at this stage?
2. Pick the **one** book idea that fits, and write the grown-up stance to *support*
   that, not steer it.
3. Keep the language a learning experience: explore, discover, notice, play, name.
4. Trace any developmental claim back to a `raw/research/` note.

See the two practical spin-off pages: [seven-life-skills](seven-life-skills.md)
and [responsive-talk](responsive-talk.md).
