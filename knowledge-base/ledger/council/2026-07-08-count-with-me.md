## Seat verdicts

### Explorer's Seat — ❌ reject
A fixed-count 'how many, tap each one' mechanic is a carpenter drilling toward a target answer, which my constitution explicitly excludes as testing/drilling rather than open-ended, child-led exploration.
**Cited:** wiki/concepts/gopnik-foundation.md — 'child as explorer... framed as a shared learning experience, not testing or drilling'; wiki/concepts/gopnik-foundation.md — 'The adult's job is to let that play run and put words to it, not to correct or redirect'; raw/research/books/gardener-and-the-carpenter.md — 'A carpenter works from a blueprint, shaping raw material toward a fixed outcome'; raw/research/books/gardener-and-the-carpenter.md — 'no quizzing, no goal but warmth and presence'
- ⚠️ The 'count' mode with a fixed `count` per round encodes a single correct answer — this is a quiz/drill toward a target, the carpenter's blueprint, not open-ended exploration.
- ⚠️ 'How many ducks? Tap each one!' directs the child toward a right-answer procedure rather than following the child's own lantern focus; the adult becomes an examiner, not a co-explorer.
- ⚠️ The mechanic treats the child as raw material to be shaped toward numeracy outcomes, contradicting the arc's core ethos that it is a practice of warmth/presence, not a child curriculum with measurable outputs.
- ⚠️ Nothing here invites the child to drop, repeat, compare, or discover on her own terms — the interaction is closed and outcome-scored.
- ✏️ Reframe from 'how many / tap each one' to open naming and following the child's focus (e.g., point-and-name whatever she fixates on, no target count), so the grown-up puts words to her play rather than testing recall.
- ✏️ If counting is desired, present it as the grown-up modeling counting aloud once and then letting the child bang/tap/re-discover freely, with no correct-count gate — matching the 'model once, then let her explore' principle.
- ✏️ Remove the implicit right-answer scoring entirely; ensure no round can be 'failed' or completed 'wrong.'

### Skills Seat — 🟡 revise
The `focus` tag is honest but the `critical-thinking` tag over-claims — a counting/tapping game does not exercise the cause-and-effect, prediction-testing 'search for reliable knowledge' that my constitution defines as Critical Thinking.
**Cited:** wiki/concepts/seven-life-skills.md (#5 Critical Thinking); raw/research/books/mind-in-the-making.md (Critical Thinking → cause-and-effect play; Focus & Self-Control); wiki/concepts/seven-life-skills.md (#1 Focus & Self-Control)
- ⚠️ The `critical-thinking` tag is not honest against my constitution. Critical Thinking in the KB is defined as 'the ongoing search for reliable knowledge' exercised through cause-and-effect play (peekaboo, drop-and-retrieve, lift-the-flap) and anticipating what happens next. A fixed-count tap-and-count game exercises one-to-one correspondence and quantity, not cause-and-effect reasoning — nothing here surprises, tests a prediction, or rewards a search for reliable knowledge. The tag over-claims.
- ⚠️ The `focus` tag IS defensible: 'Tap each one' requires sustained attention and tracking which items have been counted, which maps to Focus & Self-Control (attention, remembering, resisting distraction). This tag can stand.
- ⚠️ My constitution does not cover whether counting itself maps to a seventh skill (it doesn't appear in the seven); counting is closer to Making Connections (same/different, sorting) than to Critical Thinking — the generator may have reached for the wrong tag.
- ⚠️ Out of lens: the age (2+) sits outside the 12-month arc my constitution is written around — I abstain on age-appropriateness and defer to the Guardian/Lap seats.
- ✏️ Drop the `critical-thinking` tag — it is not exercised by a static counting task as defined in seven-life-skills.md.
- ✏️ If a second tag is wanted, consider `making-connections` (skill #4: sorting, same/different, quantity linking) which is a more honest fit for counting, OR redesign a round to genuinely exercise critical thinking (e.g. a hidden/appearing item that lets the child anticipate the next count).
- ✏️ Keep the `focus` tag — it is honestly exercised by the tap-each-one mechanic.

### Voice Seat — 🟡 revise
No bilingual claims are made so nothing is dishonest there, but the game has no grown-up cue and no serve-and-return, and its prompt positions the app as the counting voice — a social-gating failure that requires revision.
**Cited:** wiki/concepts/responsive-talk.md (cue: lines must model descriptive talk and serve-and-return pauses; a good cue tells the grown-up what to say AND to wait for her turn); raw/research/bilingual-early-years.md (social gating / Kuhl: the grown-up on the lap is the language mechanism; the app must never position itself as the teacher)
- ⚠️ The artifact contains no grown-up `cue:` line at all. The `prompt` ('How many ducks? Tap each one!') speaks directly to the child, positioning the app — not the grown-up — as the voice conducting the game. This inverts the social-gating principle: the grown-up should be the mechanism, the app should prompt the grown-up.
- ⚠️ No serve-and-return is present or supported anywhere: there is no built-in pause, no direction to the grown-up to answer the child's count/point and then wait. The counting mode as written is a child-to-screen tap loop, which is the exact anti-pattern social gating warns against.
- ⚠️ 'Tap each one!' directs the child to interact with the device rather than with the grown-up, further sidelining the dyad.
- ✏️ Add a grown-up `cue:` line for each round that models descriptive, serve-and-return talk (e.g., 'Point to each duck with her — "one… two…" — then wait for her to try.') so the grown-up voices the counting, not the app.
- ✏️ Reframe the `prompt` as an instruction to the grown-up rather than a command to the child, keeping the grown-up as the language mechanism.
- ✏️ Add an explicit pause/wait-for-her-turn beat so the claimed interaction is genuinely serve-and-return, not tap-response.

### Tone Seat — 🟡 revise
The counting prompt is structured as a test with a correct answer, which directly violates the never-test / describe-don't-judge principles; it can be salvaged by reframing to descriptive, feelings-first narration on one bounce.
**Cited:** raw/research/books/how-to-talk-little-kids.md — 'Keep cues warm and non-corrective (never test her)' / 'Describe, don't judge'; wiki/concepts/responsive-talk.md — 'Describe, don't judge', 'Keep cues warm and non-corrective (never "test" her)', 'Acknowledge feelings first', 'Serve and return … then wait'
- ⚠️ The core prompt 'How many {label}? Tap each one!' is framed as a quiz with a single correct answer — it tests the child, which my constitution explicitly forbids ('never test her'). A right/wrong count implies an implicit fail state if the child taps the wrong number.
- ⚠️ No descriptive-talk modeling: there is no grown-up cue that reports what the child actually does ('You tapped the duck!') rather than judging correctness — this misses the house style that all cue lines should be descriptive, not evaluative.
- ⚠️ No feelings-first acknowledgement and no serve-and-return pause is built into the prompt; the artifact drives toward an answer rather than honoring the child's turn.
- ⚠️ Note on scope: 'count' mode / age 2+ band may be a new mechanic-class question outside my lens — flagging for the owner/Guardian, not approving it here.
- ✏️ Reframe the prompt from an evaluative question to descriptive, playful narration: e.g. cue the grown-up to name and point ('Look — a duck… and another duck!') and celebrate the tap descriptively ('You tapped the duck!') regardless of count, so there is no correct/incorrect outcome.
- ✏️ Add an explicit grown-up cue line that acknowledges feeling and models a serve-and-return pause ('Point with her — then wait for her to tap'), per responsive-talk.md.
- ✏️ Ensure the mechanic has no scorekeeping or fail branch for a 'wrong' count; every tap should be honored warmly.

### Lap Seat — 🟡 revise
The rounds are short and the tap cue is lap-practical, but the 'How many? / count' framing turns bedtime reading into a quiz, which conflicts with the no-test, adult-stance-not-child-curriculum principles.
**Cited:** wiki/concepts/design-principles.md; wiki/concepts/the-12-month-arc.md
- ⚠️ The prompt 'How many {label}? Tap each one!' in count mode frames a right-answer quiz. At 9pm a tired grown-up with a squirming child will read this as a test and be pulled toward correcting a wrong count — this cuts against design-principles.md ('the game is forgiving — no fail states... never test' and 'never add pass/fail tasks... for the child'). The counting-quiz cadence serves an abstract instructional goal, not the warm lap dyad.
- ⚠️ Scope/rhythm: the artifact is age 2+ (2-3y, 3-4y), while the 12-month-arc (wiki/concepts/the-12-month-arc.md) frames practices for the grown-up starting at age 1 as a shared stance, not a curriculum. A numeracy 'count' mechanic reads as curriculum-for-the-child rather than an adult stance, which the arc explicitly rejects.
- ⚠️ Practicality of the count cue is otherwise fine: five short rounds (counts 2,3,3,4,2) is a sensible length, and 'Tap each one!' is a concrete, big-target-friendly gesture — no length or motion objection here.
- ✏️ Reframe the prompt away from the quiz form ('How many?') toward a shared, unfalsifiable invitation, e.g. 'Let's tap the {label} together!' — so a wrong tap wobbles and delights rather than failing the child.
- ✏️ If a counting stance is intended, express it as a grown-up practice (a `why`/stance line the adult reads) per the-12-month-arc, not as a scored task the child must get right.
- ✏️ Confirm the age band is intentional and that the mechanic still honours the no-test / no-fail floor for the older dyad; if kept at 2+, ensure the copy never implies a correct answer the parent must enforce.
