Little One — Product Plan

A gentle, growing storybook for children from about six months to five years — used as a shared lap activity: a grown-up reads aloud, the little one taps along. This page presents the two product requirement documents (PRDs) that define where the application is going, for anyone reviewing the plan.

Prepared 5 July 2026 · Source of truth: the project knowledge base (knowledge-base/ in the repository) · No child data appears anywhere in this plan.

The promises the whole plan is built on

  • Privacy by construction. A child's profile and interactions live only on the family's device — never uploaded, never in the repository. No names are ever collected.
  • The grown-up stays in the loop. This is a lap activity; the app supports the adult, it never replaces them. All guidance is a practice for the adult, not a curriculum for the child.
  • Forgiving by design. No scores to lose, no fail states, no scolding — wrong taps get a gentle nudge.
  • Evidence-based. Content and pacing trace to developmental science (Gopnik, Galinsky, Kuhl, Byers-Heinlein) recorded as sourced notes in the knowledge base.
  • Everything reviewable. The engine is simple and fixed; behavior changes are data changes — versioned, diffable, reversible.

PRD 0001 — The Adaptive Arc v1 LIVE

Make the experience adapt to this child and this family — privately, gently, and explainably.

The problem

Every child learns differently at every age; families differ in language, culture and rhythm. A single fixed menu serves the average child — which is no child.

The four pillars (all decided, all built)

🕊 Passive-first signalsQuiet observation (opens, repeats, completions). The app asks the grown-up a one-tap “noticing question” only when it has a real hypothesis — at most one per session, two per week.
🌱 Autonomy is a lifecycleWeeks 1–2 Discovery: the app explores variety across skills. Then Calibration: gentle questions begin. Then Partnership: the adult chooses “adapt automatically” or “ask me first”.
👨‍👧 The dyad, not just the childThe unit of personalisation is the parent-and-child pair, with up to two language/culture tracks for mixed-tradition homes. The app never tries to be the language teacher — the grown-up’s voice is the mechanism (Kuhl’s “social gating”).
🧭 Skills as vocabulary, never a report cardContent is tagged with Galinsky’s seven life skills and age bands; selection reasons in those terms. No meters, no grades — “moments noticed”, not scores.

What it looks like in the app today

  • A “Make it hers” setup asks only birth month and home languages — never a name.
  • The child page leads with a big “Next for …” card: the app guides to the next unfinished story instead of relying on menu choice.
  • The menu reorders per child (favourites rise, “too early” content rests, under-explored skills surface) — nothing is ever hidden.
  • Session-end noticing questions: “She keeps coming back to Animal Friends — loving it? ❤️ / 🌱 too early”. Every answer changes what is served.
  • Profile export/import for moving devices; airplane-mode works; zero network requests carry child data.

Deliberately out of v1

Weekly arc-review card · dual-language serving · any machine learning on child signals (interpretable rules only) · any backend.

PRD 0002 — The Gardener Loop BUILT · awaiting first run

A self-improving application whose learning lives in its knowledge base: an automated Claude session that proposes one reviewed improvement at a time — and learns from which proposals the owner accepts or rejects.

One pass of the Gardener

1 · Trigger. The owner presses “Run Gardener” (manual only in v1 — nothing runs on its own; $0 until pressed). Never triggered by outside input.
2 · Gather. It reads only owner-committed files: the knowledge base, the content library and its tags, a computed coverage map (age band × seven skills), and the fate of its own past proposals.
3 · Reason. It picks the single most valuable action: ingest a new research/idea note into the wiki, or write one new story/game for the biggest content gap, or a small consistency fix. Doing nothing is a valid outcome.
4 · Propose. One pull request: the change, the rationale with cited sources, and a provenance trail of what led to it.
5 · Automated checks. Machine-enforced rules: no names/PII, valid tags and art, no banned framing, the site still builds. A failing check blocks the proposal.
6 · Human gate. The owner merges (it ships) or closes (recorded rejection). The next run reads those decisions — that is the learning.

Hard rules

  • No telemetry input, ever — child data never leaves the device.
  • Content and knowledge-base changes only — never the engine, never the monthly arc (staged change-control).
  • No triggers or inputs from public issues/comments — untrusted text must not steer an automated change.
  • The Gardener’s own instructions (its charter) are a versioned file, changed only through the same reviewed process.

Why this matters beyond a storybook

The loop is a deliberate rehearsal of a real problem: workflows that must change when official guidance changes (for example, clinical protocols).

In the GardenerIn a guidance-driven workflow
Research note ingested → proposal as a reviewable diffProtocol update → exact pathway change, visible before it applies
Automated lint on every proposalConformance / terminology checks
One change per proposal; “no change” is validChange-control discipline

Git history · revertAudit trail · rollback

What “done” looks like

  • A “Run Gardener” button in the repository; pressing it yields one readable pull request (or a log explaining why none).
  • A proposal containing a name, an unknown picture, or an off-vocabulary tag cannot be merged.
  • Nothing on the live site changes until a human merges.
  • A closed proposal is demonstrably not re-proposed.

What comes after

  • Warm voice — replace the robotic device voice with pre-rendered warm narration (Claude-authored scripts + one-time neural voice render, or a family member’s recorded voice). Awaiting the owner’s choice of route.
  • Bilingual first words — naming in English + the home language on the same page, per the bilingualism evidence.
  • Gardener on a schedule — after 3–4 trusted manual runs.
  • Ages 2–5 content arcs — the tagging and coverage map already anticipate the older bands; the Gardener’s gap-filling grows the library toward them.
