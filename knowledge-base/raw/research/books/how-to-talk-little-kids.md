# Book note — How to Talk So Little Kids Will Listen

> Raw source. Faithful summary from web research (see Sources). NOTE: the book
> targets ages **2–7**, so the ~12-month uses below are deliberate *downward
> adaptations* — leaning on tone, gesture, and narration rather than the book's
> verbal scripts. No names; general guidance only.

## Bibliographic
- **Title:** *How to Talk So Little Kids Will Listen: A Survival Guide to Life with
  Children Ages 2–7*.
- **Authors:** Joanna Faber and Julie King.
- **First published:** 2017 (Scribner / Simon & Schuster).
- **Lineage:** builds on Faber & Mazlish's classic *How to Talk So Kids Will Listen*.

## Core approach
Acknowledge feelings **first**. Children cooperate once they feel understood, so
name the feeling before redirecting or problem-solving. Engage cooperation through
respect, choice, playfulness, and involvement — not control; praise by
*describing* what you see, not judging ("good job").

## Techniques (with a ~12-month adaptation)
- **Acknowledge feelings with words** — "You're sad it's gone."
  → Narrate her felt state in a simple warm tone; she reads face and voice long
    before the words. (Seeds Month 7.)
- **Give in fantasy what you can't in reality** — "I wish I could turn this into a
  real cookie!"
  → When she reaches to grab a pictured object, play along: "You want that banana!
    Yum — I'll pick it off the page." Turns frustration into a game. (Seeds Month 8.)
- **Almost-silent attention** — an interested "Mmm… ooh…" and presence.
  → Follow her point/gaze with "Ooh!" and eye contact instead of redirecting.
    (Seeds Month 6.)
- **Offer a choice** — two acceptable options.
  → Hold up two books/pages; honor whichever she reaches for. Early agency.
    (Seeds Month 9.)
- **Be playful** — silly voices, pretend, games instead of commands.
  → Voice the animals ("the cow says mooo!"), peekaboo with a flap. Play is her
    native channel. (Reinforces the game's forgiving tone.)
- **Say it with a word / give information** — one cue word, not a lecture.
  → One clear label + gesture ("gentle" while stroking the page). Matches her
    receptive-language stage.
- **Describe what you see (descriptive praise)** — report specifics, not "good boy."
  → "You turned the page! You pointed to the moon." Feeds vocabulary and shows her
    that her actions matter. (Improves the grown-up `cue:` style site-wide.)
- **Take action without insult** — act calmly, no blame.
  → If she mouths/bends the book, gently swap it while staying warm: "I'll keep
    this safe — here's a soft one." (Matches the no-scold design rule.)
